Introduction: A systematic Approach to Training Design
This document outlines the comprehensive instructional design blueprint for the PEDRRA training activity, structured according to the A.D.D.I.E. model. This framework—encompassing Analyze, Design, Develop, Implement, and Evaluate—is the established standard for effective training development and provides a systematic, evidence-based approach to creating learning experiences that are purposeful and strategically aligned with organizational needs. This introduction explains how each phase of the A.D.D.I.E. model was specifically applied to shape the PEDRRA training activity, the details of which are elaborated in the subsequent sections of this blueprint.
The Analyze phase feeds from the weaknesses identified through the Data Breach Notifications Management activity led by the TP SOTA Unit and from the feedback obtained during the 2024 Data Breach Management Awareness project. Critical performance gaps in the controller’s ability to deliver clearly the rationale behind its risk assessment output(s) are identified. Additionally, with the rising use of AI, it is observed that risk assessment is apprehended with some (unexpected) questioning and challenges by the controllers. 
In the Design phase, the above findings were translated into a strategic blueprint for the exercise. This involved crafting five SMART (Specific, Measurable, Achievable, Relevant, Time-bound) learning objectives that directly address the gaps identified during the analysis.  “An interactive and collaborative in-person classroom” format was selected to align with adult learning principles, promoting problem-centered and collaborative learning for the target audience of EUIBA professionals, along with a data breach risk assessment lecture.
The Develop phase focused on creating all the tangible instructional materials required for the exercise. This included writing different use cases serving as practical exercises to resolve collaboratively and as group works to be presented in front of the class. Key resources will also be developed, such as interactive interface to leverage participants’ engagement, comprehensive pre-and post-reading materials, solution factsheets and questionnaires to guide team discussions. 
The Implement plan details how the training will be delivered. It outlines an in-person “class-room” format for participants from EUIBAs. The plan includes facilitated plenary discussions, expert guidance from EDPS facilitators to clarify complex points and ensure learning objectives are met, and work group use case evaluations.
Finally, the Evaluate phase is designed to measure the exercise's effectiveness and envisage future improvements. This will be accomplished through the collection of anonymous participant feedback and the drafting of a comprehensive After Action Report (AAR). This report will analyze the exercise's outcomes against its objectives and provide actionable recommendations, ensuring the A.D.D.I.E. cycle continues by feeding directly into the analysis phase for the next iteration of the PEDRRA series.
Phase 1: Analyse
The Analyse phase serves as the foundation for the entire training lifecycle, as emphasized in the EDPS Guide to Effective Training. Its purpose is to ensure that the training is a necessary, relevant, and precisely targeted solution to a clearly identified problem.
Problem Identification & Training Needs Analysis (TNA)
Stemming from the analysis of the notification contents, case closure recommendations and feedback from the 2024 Awareness Program, the following identified gaps constitute the core problem that PEDRRA is designed to address:
Finding #1: Low maturity in performing data breach risk analysis - Based on the submitted notifications collected by the TP Data Breach Team, the quality of the risk assessment regularly does not meet the expected standards. Alongside not adequately defining the surface of attack (e.g., data category, data subjects’ profiles), the risk assessment rationale is not clearly laid down with properly identified adverse effects and associated increasing/decreasing factors leading to properly score the risk. In absence of such clear demonstration, the controller may certainly not meet the principle of accountability.
Finding #2: A data breach risk assessment centered on the organization - The controllers do not properly put at the center the data subjects of the assessment. Organizational risks permeate the data breach risk assessment. Such approach may lead to avoid apprehending correctly and honestly the risk score for the data subjects.
Finding #3: A high tolerance to risk - In the context of data breach risk assessment, the controller usually leans towards under-evaluating the risk level as to avoid communicating about the breach to the affected data subjects and even the authority. Controllers even develop a risk matrix where high-risk is split in two paths of decision (notify/not notify) contradicting the 3-tier risk level as described in the law.
Finding #4: Inadequate Management of Data Subject Communications – When a breach is identified as "High Risk," controllers frequently struggle to elaborate a proper communication to the affected individuals. The communication often lacks the necessary components required by Article 35, such as a clear description of the nature of the breach, the likely consequences for the data subject, and the specific mitigation measures the individual should take. There is a noticeable tendency to use overly technical or vague language, which fails to empower the data subject to protect themselves, thereby undermining the controller's accountability.
Target Audience Analysis
The training is designed for professionals from EUIBAs who are directly involved in incident response and data protection. This audience is characterized by a preference for problem-centered, experience-based learning.
Primary Roles:
Legal Teams & Senior Management: Personnel involved in strategic decision-making, risk management, and legal compliance.
Operational Managers: Managers who are responsible for the security of the assets where personal data are processed.
IT Security Personnel and data owners: Technical staff from NOC/SOC/ICT departments who are the first responders to cybersecurity incidents including Local Information Security Officers (LISO) and Local Cybersecurity Officers (LCO) responsible for security incident procedures and compliance with the Cybersecurity Regulation.
Data Protection Officers (DPOs: Staff responsible for advising on and monitoring compliance with Regulation (EU) 2018/1725 (EUDPR).
Business Continuity/Crisis Management Teams: Roles responsible for organizational resilience and response to major disruptions.
Risk officers: Risk officers analyze, measure, and mitigate potential threats—operational, financial, or strategic—that could damage an organization's assets, earnings, or reputation. They establish risk frameworks, monitor compliance, and advise leadership to ensure business continuity and regulatory adherence.
Training Goal
The overall goal of the PEDDRA exercise is to raise the capabilities of EUIs to operate a mature personal data breach risk assessment and manage the associated decision-making process expected outcomes in line with Article 34 and 35 of the Regulation.

Phase 2: Design
The Design phase acts as the architectural stage of the A.D.D.I.E. model, transforming the "what" and "why" identified during the Analyse phase into a strategic "how." As outlined in the EDPS Guide to Effective Training, this involves creating a detailed blueprint that guides all subsequent development. For PEDRRA, this meant translating the specific findings into measurable SMART learning objectives. The instructional strategy—class-room format with practical exercise—was deliberately chosen to align with adult learning principles suitable for the experienced, multi-disciplinary EUIBA’s professionals being trained. The course structure and content sequencing were meticulously planned to create a logical learning progression that directly addresses participant feedback from the previous exercise.   
Learning Objectives (SMART & Bloom´s Taxonomy. 
The following SMART (Specific, Measurable, Achievable, Relevant, Time-bound) objectives have been designed to address the identified needs:
Differentiate Organizational Risk from Data Subject Risk [Understanding / Analysis]: By the end of the "Personal Data Breach Essentials" lecture, participants will be able to distinguish between organizational impacts and adverse effects on data subjects, correctly identifying at least three specific data subject risks (e.g., identity theft, loss of control) that must be prioritized in an EUDPR-compliant assessment.
SMART Alignment: Addresses Finding #2 (organization-centered risk) by refocusing the assessment lens.
Apply EDPS Methodology [Apply / Analyse]: Following the "Personal Data Breach Essentials" lecture, participants will apply the EDPS-standard risk assessment methodology to three distinct data breach use cases, identifying at least 80% of the key risks to data subjects and integrating these findings into a standardized risk assessment template.
SMART Alignment: Directly addresses Finding #1 (low maturity in methodology) and Finding #2 (neglecting data subject impact) by requiring a high degree of accuracy in risk identification.
Formulate a Risk Assessment Rationale [Create/Evaluate]: During the "Use Case Exercise," participants will construct a written rationale for a risk score (Low, Medium, High) that explicitly maps the "surface of attack" to specific increasing or decreasing risk factors as defined in EDPS guidelines.
SMART Alignment: Addresses Finding #1 regarding the inability to deliver clearly the rationale behind risk assessment output.
Determine Notification Obligations [Evaluate]: Given a complex data breach use case, participants will decide whether a breach requires notification to the EDPS and/or communication to data subjects within the legal 72-hour framework, justifying the decision based on the 3-tier risk level described in the law.
SMART Alignment: Addresses Finding #3 (high risk tolerance and incorrect decision paths).
Critically Review Peer Risk Assessments [Evaluate]: During the "Peer-Review" session, participants will critique the risk assessment output of another group, identifying at least one potential under-evaluation of risk or one missing data subject profile using the provided rubric.
SMART Alignment: Addresses Finding #3. Encourages collaborative learning and corrects the "under-evaluation" bias identified in the analysis.
Manage Data Subject Communication [Create/Evaluate]: For breaches identified as high-risk, participants will draft a formal notification to data subjects that includes all mandatory Article 35 elements—the nature of the breach, likely consequences, and mitigation steps—using clear, non-technical language. 
SMART Alignment: Addresses Finding #4. Corrects the difficulty controllers face in elaborating proper, transparent communications that empower data subjects..
Instructional Strategy
PEDRRA will employ a multi-faceted instructional strategy, combining several methods to create a comprehensive and engaging learning experience. This blended approach is designed to cater to diverse learning preferences and address the specific needs as identified through.  
The core of the training is a “in-person classroom geared towards learning by examples” framed with active interaction, problem-centered, and collaborative learning—key tenets of adult learning theory. The format requires participants to apply their knowledge to realistic situations based on individual and collaborative participation.  
This experiential learning is strategically supplemented by other instructional methods:
Direct Instruction: A formal lecture on "Personal Data Breach Essentials" and “Risk Assessment Framework” will be delivered to provide foundational knowledge and learn by experience (CEPOL Testimony).  
Collaborative Learning: Facilitated plenary discussions after each example and a comprehensive debriefing session at the end of the day will allow participants to share their approaches, learn from diverse perspectives, and collectively construct a deeper understanding of best practices.  
Case-Based Learning: A dedicated session for the realization of the mandatory group works stemming from the provided use cases will be included to connect theoretical concepts to real-world application.  
Expert Guidance: Throughout the exercise, EDPS facilitators will provide continuous expert guidance and coaching, clarifying complex points and reinforcing best practices in real-time.  
Technology-Enhanced Learning: To complement the use case-based discussions, the training will utilize a custom web-based application, the "EUDPR Training Platform," to drive engagement and provide immediate performance data. This tool employs gamification elements (XP, badges) to foster friendly competition and utilizes instant feedback loops to reinforce learning objectives in real-time.
This combination of strategies ensures that participants not only engage in hands-on problem-solving but also receive the necessary theoretical grounding and expert reinforcement to build lasting capabilities.
Course Structure & Sequencing
The 6-hour training is structured to build knowledge and skills, following a detailed agenda:
Welcome and Introduction: Sets the context.
Lecture: "Personal Data Breach Essentials: EDPS Perspective, Risk Assessment, and Notification" (New data DB risk assessment tool): This lecture, directly addresses participants' requests for "more explicit guidance from the EDPS" and an "introduction to the topic 'Data Breaches' from the perspective of the EDPS". It provides the foundational knowledge needed for the more complex subsequent use cases. CEPOL will present their experience following the 2024 data breach. 
Interactive quiz - During this section, the participants will be asked to participate in a quiz. The examples will be the object of further debrief.
Intermediary wrap-up - This section will include plenary discussion about the information and explanations provided.
Use case exercise - Hands-on practice on a given use case. Participants will be demanded to produce a risk assessment on the provided data breach use case (Review of real-world case studies from EDPS guidelines to solidify understanding of risk levels and notification decisions.)
Peer-Review - The participants collectively will review the work of 2 groups. The other work group risk assessment will be corrected post-training.
Overall Training Debrief and Feedback: A final session for reflection, feedback collection, and discussion of future training needs and improvements.
Assessment & Evaluation Methods
The evaluation will be multi-faceted, incorporating Kirkpatrick's Four-Level Model to measure effectiveness comprehensively:
Level 1 (Reaction): An anonymous feedback form will be used to gather participant satisfaction with the content, facilitation, and overall experience.
Level 2 (Learning): Team responses to the use cases questionnaires will be analyzed to assess the acquisition of knowledge and the ability to apply risk assessment and coordination principles.
Level 3 (Behavior): The EDPS will usethe post-exercise After Action Report (AAR) to assess this level, which will provide actionable recommendations designed to influence and improve actual work practices and internal processes within EUIBAs.
Level 4 (Results): The long-term impact will be measured by the adoption of the AAR recommendations and a potential reduction in compliance issues or improved breach notifications from participating EUIBAs in the future.
Phase 3: Develop
The Develop phase is the production stage of the A.D.D.I.E. model, where the design blueprint is brought to life through the creation of all learning materials and resources. This phase moves from planning to creation, ensuring that every element is crafted to be accurate, engaging, and effective. For PEDRRA, this involved more than just preparing presentations; it meant developing complex, realistic examples and specific pedagogical tools, such as mock-up RoPAS or risk assessment templates, to create a more challenging and practical learning experience. Every resource, from pre-reading packages to facilitator guides, is developed in this phase to support the learning objectives and instructional strategy. 
Training Materials Development
The following materials will be created and compiled:
Use Case Descriptions.
Participant Workbooks: Including questionnaires for the use cases, mock-up RoPA, mock-up DPIA.
Facilitator's Guide: A comprehensive guide for facilitators with presentation slides, use cases, timing cues, key discussion prompts, and expected learning outcomes.
Pre-reading Package: A curated set of documents for participants to review beforehand, including:
Excerpts from Regulation (EU) 2018/1725 (EUDPR) and Regulation (EU, Euratom) 2023/2841 (Cybersecurity Regulation).
Relevant EDPS and EDPB guidelines on personal data breach notification.
Role-specific information sheets to simulate realistic information silos and compel collaboration.
Practical Tools:
Risk assessment templates, for personal data breach accessible from the online platform used during the seminar.
Real-case examples from EDPS guidelines for analysis.
A base glossary for the terminology alignment activity.
Evaluation Instruments: An anonymous feedback form for post-exercise data collection, along with rubrics for evaluating the quality of the developed glossaries, process maps, and communication protocols.
Activity Development
The core learning activities will be developed to be highly interactive and collaborative:
Examples-Solving: For each example, participants will be requested to provide an answer, possibly debate about it.
Facilitated Plenary Discussions: Following each team session, a facilitator will lead a group discussion, inviting teams to share their findings and debating different approaches to foster peer-to-peer learning.
Collaborative Development Sessions: Dedicated time for teams to work on the use case to put into practice what they learned during the morning sessions.
Final Debriefing Session: An open forum for participants to reflect on key takeaways, discuss challenges in inter-role collaboration, and provide suggestions for future training.
Quality Assurance (QA) & Piloting
To ensure the training is factually accurate, technically viable, and pedagogically effective, a rigorous Quality Assurance (QA) process will be implemented prior to the full rollout.
Subject Matter Expert (SME) Review 
Before the materials are finalized, a targeted review will be conducted to validate the realism and legal accuracy of the content.
Legal Validation: A senior Legal Officer will review the "Solution Keys" for the second use case ("The Inter-Agency Data Leak") to ensure the interpretation of "High Risk" regarding the health status indicators aligns strictly with EDPS guidelines and current case law.
Technical Validation: An IT Security expert will review the technical details of the use cases and examples used in the training and for the work groups.
Technology Stress Test
Given the reliance on the "EUDPR Training Platform" for engagement, a specific technical QA phase is required.
Functionality Check: The development team will verify that the gamification logic (awarding XP and Badges) triggers correctly upon answer submission and that the "Leaderboard" visualizes correctly on the main presentation screen.
Load Testing: A connectivity test will be performed at the physical venue to ensure the Wi-Fi bandwidth can support simultaneous app usage by all participants without latency.
The Pilot Test (Dry Run)
A full "dress rehearsal" will be conducted with a small, representative group (6-8 participants) comprising at least one IT staff member, one DPO, and one LCO. The pilot will focus on:
Use Case and Quiz Dynamics: validating that the use cases designed successfully triggers the intended debate between participants, rather than immediate consensus.
Timing Calibration: Confirming that the different designed activities can be realistically completed within the allocated 6-hour window.
Facilitator Protocol: Testing the effectiveness of the facilitators soft skills, training capabilities and mechanism to resolve deadlocked debates.
Iterative Refinement
Following the pilot, a "Hot Wash" debrief will be held to gather qualitative feedback. Any friction points—such as confusing wording in the app questions or unrealistic timing in the lecture—will be corrected in the materials before the final "Go/No-Go" decision for implementation.
Phase 4: Implement
The Implement phase is the "live" execution stage of the A.D.D.I.E. model, where the meticulously planned and developed training is delivered to participants. As emphasized in the EDPS Guide to Effective Training, successful implementation requires careful management of the learning environment and the crucial role of the facilitator. For PEDRRA, this is not a passive presentation but a dynamic, facilitated exercise. The in-person, team-based format is designed to simulate real-world collaboration within EUIBAs. A critical component of implementation is the creation of a psychologically safe environment, a key tenet of adult learning, to encourage open and honest discussion about procedural weaknesses without fear of judgment.  
Rollout Plan
Format: A 6-hour, in-person training event conducted in a single day.
Environment: A venue conducive to both small team breakout work and larger plenary discussions.
Schedule: The training will follow the detailed session breakdown outlined in the Design phase.
Participant Onboarding: Pre-reading materials and logistical information will be distributed to registered participants in advance to ensure they arrive prepared. 
Facilitators' Responsibilities
EDPS facilitators will be central to the exercise's success. Their responsibilities include:
Delivering the introduction and presenting the use cases clearly.
Guiding teams during breakout sessions, answering clarifying questions, and ensuring they remain on task.
Leading the plenary discussions, encouraging participation from all roles, and highlighting key learning points.
Providing expert guidance on EDPS's perspective, particularly regarding risk assessment methodologies and notification obligations.
Managing time effectively to ensure the agenda is followed.
Facilitating the final debriefing session and encouraging candid feedback.
Undergoing specific training on the new collaborative activities and how to facilitate them effectively.
Creating a “Safe Space”
Chatham House Rule: Explicitly state that the session operates under non-attribution rules to encourage honest discussion about internal process failures.
"No Blame" Policy: Instruct facilitators to frame incorrect answers as "learning opportunities" rather than compliance failures.
Technology Used
Presentation Software: For delivering use case information and lecture content.
Online Survey Tools: For the efficient and anonymous collection of participant feedback at the conclusion of the exercise.
Collaborative Digital Tools: For real-time development of glossaries, process maps, and communication protocols (e.g., shared documents, digital whiteboards).
Delivery Logistics
Venue: A physical location will be secured that can accommodate the required number of participants and allows for both plenary sessions and separate tables for team-based work.
Team Composition: Participants will be grouped by their EUIBA of origin to simulate real-world internal collaboration dynamics.
Materials: All necessary materials (workbooks, pens, pre-reading printouts) will be provided to participants on-site. 
 
Phase 5: Evaluate
The Evaluate phase is the final and cyclical component of the A.D.D.I.E. model, crucial for measuring impact and driving continuous improvement, as highlighted in the EDPS Guide to Effective Training. For PEDRRA, evaluation extends far beyond simple satisfaction surveys. It employs the multi-level Kirkpatrick Model to systematically measure outcomes, from immediate participant reactions (Level 1) and knowledge acquisition (Level 2) to tangible changes in workplace behaviour (Level 3) and organizational resilience (Level 4). The key output, the After Action Report, is designed not merely to document the event but to provide actionable recommendations for EUIBAs and to serve as the foundational analysis for the next iteration of the PEDRRA series, thus completing and restarting the A.D.D.I.E. cycle.
Feedback Collection Methods
Immediate Feedback: An anonymous feedback form will be administered at the end of the session to capture Level 1 (Reaction) data on content relevance, facilitation quality, and overall satisfaction.
Learning Assessment: The completed questionnaires from the use case sessions will be collected and analysed to measure Level 2 (Learning) outcomes, assessing the quality of risk assessments and the coherence of response plans.
Behavioral Change Assessment: A follow-up survey will be sent to participants and their managers three months after the training to assess the application of learned skills and the implementation of developed resources (Level 3).
Evaluation Metrics
Participant Satisfaction: Quantitative ratings and qualitative comments from the feedback forms.
Knowledge & Skill Application: Quality and completeness of team responses, demonstrating understanding of risk assessment, notification timelines, and inter-role coordination.
Identified Process Gaps: Analysis of participant discussions and AAR feedback to identify common challenges and areas for improvement within EUIBA processes.
Behavioural Change: Self-reported changes in on-the-job behaviour and collaboration patterns from the follow-up survey.
Organizational Impact: Long-term metrics such as a reduction in the time to notify the EDPS of a breach, an increase in the quality of notifications, and a decrease in reported inter-departmental friction during incident response (Level 4).
Improvement Strategy
The evaluation culminates in the creation of a comprehensive After Action Report (AAR).
Analysis: The AAR will synthesize all collected data, including team responses and feedback, to identify key findings, successes, and areas for improvement.
Recommendations: The report will provide concrete, actionable recommendations for participating EUIBAs to enhance their internal processes, communication protocols, and staff training (Level 3 - Behavior).
Continuous Improvement Cycle: The findings and recommendations from the PEDRRA AAR will serve as the primary input for the Analyze phase of the next iteration of the PEDRRA II exercise, ensuring the program evolves to meet the changing needs of the EUIBAs and the evolving threat landscape.

ANNEX I: Lesson Plan
Part I: General Information
1. Title: PEDRRA: PErsonal Data bReach Risk Assessment 
2. Date: 2026
3. Target Audience: This exercise is designed for staff from European Union Institutions, Bodies, and Agencies (EUIBAs). Key roles expected to participate include:
IT Managers and IT Security Personnel (e.g., from NOC / SOC / ICT departments), including Delegated Controllers for IT systems.
Security Officers (e.g., Local Information Security Officers (LISO), Local Cybersecurity Officers (LCO)).
Data Protection Officers (DPOs) and Data Protection Coordinators/Contacts (DPCs).
Legal teams and Senior Management.
Business Continuity/Crisis Management teams (as per new scenario requests).
4. Duration: Approximately 6 hours (09:30 – 16:00), divided into two sessions, with a strong emphasis on interactive discussion and team-based problem-solving.
5. Instructional Designers: Systems Oversight and Technology Audits Sector (T&P Unit).
6. Method: Job Aids, Demonstration, Guided Discovery (TTX).
Part II: Goals and Objectives
1. Instructional Goal: To raise awareness of personal data breach management among EUIBAs staff, fostering the inter-disciplinary collaboration required to effectively manage a personal data breach that originates from a cybersecurity incident.
2. Learning Objectives (PEDRRA): Based on the analysis of PATRICIA II, the following objectives are specifically designed for PEDRRA to address identified needs and enhance participant capabilities:
Objective 1. Enhance Practical Skills in Personal Data Breach Risk Assessment: By the end of the session, participants will be able to apply a provided EDPS-standard risk assessment tool? to a complex breach scenario, correctly identifying at least 80% of the key risks to data subjects' rights and freedoms. 
This refinement introduces a specific tool (the template or the DB risk assessment tool), a measurable performance standard (80% accuracy), and a clear, time-bound context (by the end of the session).
Objective 2. Standardize Inter-disciplinary Communication: During the exercise, teams will produce a documented communication plan for a simulated breach that correctly identifies all mandatory reporting obligations under both the EUDPR and the Cybersecurity Regulation, including timelines and responsible internal roles, with no critical omissions. 
This shifts the focus from a general goal of "improvement" to the creation of a measurable output (a documented plan) that can be assessed against a clear standard.
Objective 3. Enhance Strategic Decision-Making and Multi-Stakeholder Engagement in Complex Scenarios: When presented with a complex scenario inject, each team's designated senior management representative will verbally articulate a decision on whether to communicate the breach to data subjects and provide a concise justification (under 2 minutes) based on the 'high risk' threshold from Article 35 of the EUDPR , referencing at least two key factors from the scenario. 
This objective is measured by the drafting of the decision that must meet clear content and justification criteria, simulating a real-world task.
Objective 4. Develop Targeted Resources: By the end of the exercise, each interdisciplinary team will identify and list their top two most critical knowledge gaps observed during the scenarios (e.g., 'LCOs need a better understanding of 'high risk' criteria,' or 'DPOs need a primer on ransomware forensics'). For each gap, they will propose one concrete resource (e.g., a one-page checklist, a 30-minute webinar topic) that would help bridge it. This output will be collected and used to inform post-exercise resource development.
This makes the objective of "identifying needs" a measurable action with a clear output (a list of gaps and proposed resources) that directly contributes to future training development.
3. Key Regulations and Guidelines Referenced:

ANNEX II: Scenario 1. The Encrypted Gateway (Stolen Laptop)
Pedagogical Goal
To present a personal data breach incident that is ambiguous between “unlikely risk” and “risk” to data subjects, prompting participants to critically evaluate the effectiveness of security measures, the role of contractual safeguards, and the threshold for notification. This scenario sets the stage for the subsequent lecture on comprehensive risk assessment.
Context
Your European Union Institution, Body, or Agency (EUIBA), referred to as “EU Connect,” operates under Regulation (EU) 2018/1725 (EUDPR) and Regulation (EU, Euratom) 2023/2841 (Cybersecurity Regulation). EU Connect has well-defined internal IT security procedures, a Data Protection Officer (DPO), a Local Cybersecurity Officer (LCO), and an established internal incident response procedure.
EU Connect uses external consultants for certain projects. All consultants operate under a service contract that includes a Data Processing Agreement (DPA) pursuant to Article 29 EUDPR. The standard DPA requires, among other things: (a) full-disk encryption on any device used to access or store EU Connect data; (b) the use of EU Connect’s Virtual Private Network (VPN) for remote system access; (c) that personal data is not stored locally on personal devices beyond what is strictly necessary for the task; and (d) immediate notification to the EU Connect IT Security team of any security incident involving EU Connect data.
Incident Description: 
Incident timeline:
Day 1, 08:15 UTC  An external consultant (a contractor, not EU Connect staff) who works remotely discovers that their personal laptop was stolen from their locked home office overnight during a burglary. The consultant has filed a police report.
Day 1, 09:45 UTC  The consultant calls the Head of Unit for the “UnionGate” project to report the theft. The consultant is distraught but emphasises the laptop was locked and encrypted.
Day 1, 10:00 UTC  The Head of Unit informs the IT Security team.
Day 1, 10:30 UTC  The IT Security team’s initial technical assessment is available (see below).
Initial Technical Findings (Day 1, 10:30 UTC):
Device Security:
The consultant confirms the laptop had full-disk encryption (BitLocker) enabled — a mandatory security measure under the DPA. They state they used a strong, unique passphrase (20+ characters, mixed case/numbers/symbols, not a dictionary word, not reused from other services).
The EU Connect IT Security team immediately initiates a remote wipe command via the Mobile Device Management (MDM) platform. The command is confirmed as “sent” by the management software. The status of “successful execution” is pending — it requires the device to connect to the internet, which it has not done since the theft.
Data Involved:
The laptop contained a local copy of a project dataset from the “UnionGate” platform. This dataset consists of names, professional email addresses, and institutional affiliations of approximately 150 external experts involved in an EU Connect-hosted workshop. The data is classified as personal data but does not include special categories of personal data (e.g., health data, financial data, or national ID numbers).
The consultant confirms they downloaded the dataset 10 days ago to prepare name badges for the workshop. The DPA permits temporary local storage for specific, justified tasks, but requires deletion within 30 days of task completion. The workshop took place 5 days ago. The dataset had not yet been deleted from the laptop.
Availability & Backups:
EU Connect maintains up-to-date backups of all project data on its internal, secured servers. The data is not permanently lost to EU Connect (no permanent availability breach for the controller).
Threat Assessment:
There is no immediate evidence or indication that the consultant’s passphrase has been compromised (no brute-force attempts on other accounts, no phishing linked to the burglary), nor any known exploited vulnerabilities in the specific BitLocker implementation on the stolen laptop’s OS version.
The nature of the theft (burglary from a locked home office; other items including electronics were also taken) suggests opportunistic theft rather than a targeted cyber-attack to acquire data.
The police report confirms forced entry through a window. No forensic evidence suggests the laptop was specifically targeted.
📋 Facilitator Note: The combination of encryption + strong passphrase + opportunistic theft + backups is designed to push towards “unlikely risk.” The pending remote wipe + physical possession lost + time-to-deletion uncertainty pushes towards “risk.” Watch for teams that assume encryption is a complete shield — push them to consider what happens if the passphrase is eventually compromised or the encryption is bypassed.

ANNEX III: Scenario 2. The Inter-Agency Data Leak (Cloud Service Provider Compromise)
Pedagogical Goal
To create a complex scenario that forces genuine debate between “risk” and “high risk” thresholds, incorporating sensitive data, multi-party coordination obligations under both the EUDPR and the Cybersecurity Regulation, processor accountability, and data retention violations. This scenario challenges participants to conduct a complete risk assessment and coordinate across institutional boundaries.
Context
Your EUIBA (“EUIBA-Alpha”) is the lead institution on a large-scale public health research project studying population health trends across the European Union. EUIBA-Alpha collaborates with another Union Institution (“EUIBA-Beta”), which holds critical re-identification keys. Both operate under the EUDPR and the Cybersecurity Regulation.
Contractual and Organisational Setup:
EUIBA-Alpha is the controller for this processing activity.
EUIBA-Beta is not a co-controller. It acts solely as a custodian of the re-identification keys under a specific inter-institutional agreement. EUIBA-Beta has no access to the research data itself and no role in determining the purposes or means of processing.
“CloudVault Solutions” is a third-party cloud service provider acting as a processor under a Data Processing Agreement (DPA) pursuant to Article 29 EUDPR. The DPA specifies, among other things: (a) encryption at rest for all stored data using AES-256; (b) access logging and real-time intrusion detection; (c) immediate notification to EUIBA-Alpha of any security incident affecting their data; and (d) annual security audits by an independent third party.
EUIBA-Alpha’s RoPA (Record of Processing Activities) documents this processing, and a DPIA was conducted 4 years ago when the project launched. The DPIA is due for review.
📋 Facilitator Note: The improved scenario clarifies the legal relationships upfront. EUIBA-Beta is NOT a co-controller (the original was ambiguous). CloudVault’s contractual obligations are now explicit, which creates the accountability discussion around the encryption failure.
Incident Description
Incident timeline:
Day 1, 14:00 UTC  EUIBA-Alpha’s Local Cybersecurity Officer (LCO) receives an urgent alert from CERT-EU indicating a confirmed major security incident involving CloudVault Solutions. CERT-EU classifies this as a “significant incident” under Article 19 of the Cybersecurity Regulation and requests immediate coordination.
Day 1, 15:30 UTC  CloudVault’s incident response team contacts EUIBA-Alpha’s IT Security team directly, confirming their infrastructure was breached. CloudVault states this notification is made pursuant to their obligations under Article 33(2) EUDPR as a processor, and under clause 8.3 of the DPA.
Day 1, 17:00 UTC  CloudVault provides a detailed technical assessment (see below).
Day 1, 19:00 UTC  EUIBA-Alpha’s internal investigation confirms the affected storage buckets correspond to the public health research project database.
Detailed Technical Findings (Day 1, 17:00 UTC):
Compromise Scope
Attackers gained unauthorised access to specific storage buckets on CloudVault used by EUIBA-Alpha for the public health research project. The attack vector was a sophisticated spear-phishing campaign targeting CloudVault system administrators, which led to compromised administrative credentials.
Forensic analysis, confirmed by CloudVault’s external incident response firm, indicates that data exfiltration occurred over a 48-hour period prior to detection. The attackers used encrypted channels to extract data, making it difficult to determine the exact volume exfiltrated.
Attribution analysis by CERT-EU suggests the attack was conducted by a suspected state-sponsored cyber-espionage group known to target EU institutional data.
Data Involved 
The compromised database contains 750,000 records of EU citizens participating in the research study. Each record includes:
A pseudonymised identifier generated by a one-way cryptographic hash of the participant’s national ID number.
Demographic data: age group (e.g., 18–25, 26–35), gender, and country of residence.
A “health status indicator” (values: “Good,” “Fair,” “Poor”) derived from self-reported general health questionnaires. While this is not an individual medical diagnosis, it constitutes data concerning health within the meaning of Article 10 EUDPR and Recital 35 of the GDPR, as it reveals information about an individual’s health status.
📋 Facilitator Note: The health status indicator classification is now stated explicitly rather than left ambiguous. However, the facilitator should expect and welcome debate from participants who argue that a generalised “Good/Fair/Poor” indicator for pseudonymised records is not truly “data concerning health.” The key point to reinforce is that EUDPR/GDPR adopts a BROAD interpretation: any data that reveals health information, however general, falls within the definition. Push teams to consider what happens if this data is re-identified.
Re-identification Key
The original national ID numbers and the unique hashing key required to reverse the pseudonymisation are held exclusively by EUIBA-Beta on a separate, highly secured internal system, completely isolated from CloudVault. There is no evidence of any compromise of EUIBA-Beta’s systems or the key itself.
However, the demographic combination (age group + gender + country of residence) could potentially serve as a quasi-identifier. Research has shown that combinations of three or more demographic attributes can re-identify individuals in large datasets with varying degrees of success, particularly for residents of smaller EU Member States or less common demographic combinations.
Contractual Breach
EUIBA-Alpha’s post-incident review of CloudVault’s security configuration reveals that encryption at rest was NOT enabled for the affected storage buckets, despite being a mandatory requirement under clause 5.2(a) of the DPA and a condition identified as essential in EUIBA-Alpha’s DPIA. CloudVault has not yet provided an explanation for this discrepancy.
CloudVault’s most recent independent security audit (conducted 8 months ago) did not flag this configuration gap.
Data Retention Oversight
EUIBA-Alpha’s data retention policy for this pseudonymised dataset specifies a maximum storage period of 5 years. Internal checks (initiated after the CloudVault alert) reveal that data from the project’s pilot phase, dating back 7 years, was still present in the compromised database due to an oversight in automated deletion scripts. This means an additional 2 years of historical data, including health status indicators, were exposed beyond their intended retention period.
This constitutes a potential violation of the storage limitation principle under Article 4(1)(e) EUDPR, compounding the breach with an accountability issue that pre-dates the security incident.

SECTION 1: RISK ASSESSMENT PRIMER
Distribute to participants before Scenario 1 begins (during Welcome session). This 2-page handout provides the minimum framework needed to work through the scenarios.
Purpose
This primer gives you the essential risk assessment framework you will apply during the scenarios.
The full lecture on Personal Data Breach Essentials follows Scenario 1 and provides deeper context.
For now, focus on understanding the 3-tier risk framework and the key assessment factors.
1.1 What Is a Personal Data Breach?
A personal data breach is “a breach of security leading to the accidental or unlawful destruction, loss, alteration, unauthorised disclosure of, or access to, personal data” (Article 3(16) EUDPR).
Three types:
Type
Meaning
Example
Confidentiality breach
Unauthorised disclosure or access
Data sent to wrong recipient; stolen laptop with data
Integrity breach
Unauthorised alteration
Database records modified by attacker
Availability breach
Loss of access to data
Ransomware encrypts files; only copy destroyed
A single incident can involve multiple types simultaneously.
1.2 The 3-Tier Risk Framework
Every personal data breach must be assessed against this framework:
Risk Level
What It Means
Notification Required
Unlikely Risk
The breach is unlikely to result in a risk to the rights and freedoms of data subjects.
Internal record only (Art. 34(5)). No EDPS notification. No data subject communication.
Risk
The breach is likely to result in a risk, but not a high risk.
Notify the EDPS within 72 hours (Art. 34). No data subject communication.
High Risk
The breach is likely to result in a HIGH risk to the rights and freedoms of data subjects.
Notify the EDPS within 72 hours (Art. 34) AND communicate to data subjects without undue delay (Art. 35).
1.3 Key Assessment Factors
When assessing risk, consider each of these factors from the EDPS Guidelines:
Factor
What to Ask
Higher Risk If...
Type of breach
Confidentiality, integrity, or availability?
Confidentiality (data in wrong hands)
Nature of data
What categories of personal data are affected?
Special categories (health, political opinions, etc.)
Sensitivity
How sensitive is the data in context?
Data that could cause discrimination or stigma
Volume
How many records / data subjects affected?
Large numbers of individuals
Ease of identification
Can data subjects be identified from the data?
Directly identifiable (name, email, ID number)
Severity of consequences
What could happen to the data subjects?
Identity theft, discrimination, physical danger
Special characteristics
Are affected individuals vulnerable?
Children, patients, whistleblowers
Nature of threat actor
Who has the data now?
Sophisticated/motivated actor vs. opportunistic
Mitigating measures
Was the data protected (encryption, pseudonymisation)?
No encryption; data in clear text
Key Principle
The risk assessment must focus on the data SUBJECTS, not on the organisation.
Ask: “What could happen to the PEOPLE whose data was compromised?” — not “What could happen to our institution?”
This is the most common error identified in EDPS supervision (Finding #2).
1.4 The 72-Hour Clock
If the breach is assessed as “Risk” or “High Risk,” the controller must notify the EDPS without undue delay and, where feasible, not later than 72 hours after becoming “aware.”
When does “awareness” start? When the controller has a “reasonable degree of certainty” that personal data has been compromised. This is not when the DPO is formally told — it is when the institution, through any of its staff, gains that certainty.

Facilitator Instructions

🛡 DPO LEAD
Data Protection Officer perspective
Your responsibilities in this scenario:
You LEAD the risk assessment. Ensure the team considers all EDPS guideline factors.
You decide the notification chain: who must be informed, in what order, within what timeframe.
You draft the notification decision: Unlikely Risk / Risk / High Risk — with written justification.
You ensure the assessment focuses on DATA SUBJECTS, not on the organisation.
If the team concludes High Risk, you lead the Article 35 communication draft.
Remember: You are the controller’s conscience. If something feels like it’s being under-evaluated, push back.
🔧 TECHNICAL LEAD (LCO / IT Security)
Local Cybersecurity Officer / IT Security perspective
Your responsibilities in this scenario:
You LEAD the technical analysis: what happened, what data was affected, what protections were in place.
You assess the effectiveness of security measures (encryption, remote wipe, pseudonymisation).
You identify what technical information is missing and who should provide it.
You coordinate with CERT-EU obligations under the Cybersecurity Regulation.
You evaluate the threat actor: opportunistic vs. targeted vs. state-sponsored.
Remember: Be honest about what encryption can and cannot protect against. Don’t overstate technical safeguards.
🏛 DECISION-MAKER (Senior Management)
Strategic decision-making perspective
Your responsibilities in this scenario:
You make the FINAL call on the risk level and notification decision, based on the team’s analysis.
You must verbally present your team’s verdict and justification in the plenary (under 2 minutes).
You consider: organisational accountability, reputational implications, and the 72-hour clock.
You challenge the team if the rationale is weak: “Can we defend this to the EDPS?”
You ensure the risk assessment is documented, not just discussed.
Remember: You are accountable. “I didn’t know” is not a defence. Ask the hard questions now.
📜 LEGAL / COMMUNICATIONS
Legal compliance and data subject communication perspective
Your responsibilities in this scenario:
You verify the legal basis for each notification obligation (Articles 33, 34, 35 EUDPR).
You assess processor accountability: what does the DPA require? Was it followed?
You lead the data subject communication draft (Article 35 elements).
You ensure communications use clear, non-technical language that empowers data subjects.
You flag any additional violations (data retention, storage limitation, RoPA completeness).
Remember: Your job is to make the complex simple. If a data subject can’t understand the communication, it fails.

The pilot test is a full dress rehearsal of the PEDRRA training with a small, representative group. Its purpose is to validate that all materials, activities, technology, and facilitation protocols work as intended before the full rollout.

3.2 Mandatory Technology Stress Test
During the Practical Examples session (14:30–15:00), the pilot must include a deliberate technology failure test:
Analog Fallback Test Protocol
At 14:45 (15 minutes into the quiz session): the facilitator announces: “We have a technical issue with the app. Please put down your devices. We’re switching to the backup method.”
The facilitator then:
1. Distributes the A/B/C/D cards and colour risk cards from the Analog Kit.
2. Reads the next quiz question aloud.
3. Teams hold up answer cards. Facilitator tallies on flip chart.
Time this transition. Target: complete switch within 3 minutes. If it takes longer, the Analog Kit design needs revision.
After 2 analog questions, the facilitator can “restore” the app if time permits.

5. Hot Wash Debrief Protocol
The Hot Wash is a structured debrief held immediately after the pilot (16:15–16:45). It captures qualitative feedback while the experience is fresh.
5.1 Ground Rules
Candour is essential — we need honest feedback, not politeness.
Everything said is for improvement purposes only — no attribution in the report.
Focus on what can be fixed, not what went perfectly.
Every participant speaks at least once.
5.2 Structured Questions
The facilitator leads the debrief using these questions. Allow 3–4 minutes per question.
Round 1: Overall Impression (5 min)
Q1: In one word, describe your overall experience of the training today.
Go around the table. Note the words. This gives an immediate temperature check.
Round 2: Materials & Clarity (5 min)
Q2: Was there any point where you were confused by the instructions, the workbook, or the scenario materials? Be specific.
Q3: Was the pre-reading package useful? Did it prepare you adequately?
Round 2b: Enhancement Materials (3 min)
Q3b: Did the Risk Assessment Primer help you get started with Scenario 1, or was it unnecessary?
Q3c: Did the Team Role Cards affect how your team worked together? Positively or negatively?
Q3d: Was the pre-filled Communication Template helpful, or would you have preferred a blank slate?
Round 3: Scenarios & Activities (8 min)
Q4: Did Scenario 1 (Stolen Laptop) feel realistic? Was it too easy, too hard, or about right?
Q5: Did Scenario 2 (Cloud Leak) force a genuine “Risk vs. High Risk” debate in your team? What was the hardest part?
Q6: Was the data subject communication exercise (15 min) useful? Was 15 minutes enough?
Q7: Did the inject cards change your team’s assessment? Did you feel the timing was right?
Round 4: Technology & Logistics (5 min)
Q8: How was the app experience? Easy to use? Any glitches?
Q9: Did the gamification (XP, badges, leaderboard) add to the experience or distract from it?
Q10: Were there any logistics issues (room, materials, timing, breaks)?
Round 5: Facilitation (4 min)
Q11: Did you feel comfortable speaking up, even if your view differed from senior colleagues?
Q12: Was the facilitator’s guidance helpful without being directive?
Round 6: Final (3 min)
Q13: If you could change one thing about this training, what would it be?
Q14: Would you recommend this training to colleagues at your EUIBA?

Gamification App • Quiz Engine • Leaderboard • Analog Fallback

1. Purpose & Scope
The EUDPR Training Platform is a web-based application used during the PEDRRA training to drive participant engagement, deliver interactive quizzes, and collect real-time performance data. It supplements the core scenario-based exercises (which are designed to work without technology) with gamification elements.
Design Principle: Analog-First
The PEDRRA programme is designed so that all core learning objectives can be achieved without the app.
The app enhances engagement but is never a single point of failure.
Every app feature must have a documented analog fallback (see Section 7).

Facilitator creates a “session” with a unique 6-character alphanumeric join code.
Participants join by entering the code on their device (phone, tablet, or laptop) — no app installation required (progressive web app or simple URL).
Facilitator can pause, resume, and end the session at any time.
Session supports 5–50 simultaneous participants.

1. EUDPR: Personal Data Breach Provisions
Regulation (EU) 2018/1725 — the key articles you will apply during the training.
Article 3(16): Definition
“Personal data breach” means a breach of security leading to the accidental or unlawful destruction, loss, alteration, unauthorised disclosure of, or access to, personal data transmitted, stored or otherwise processed.
Key point: Not every security incident is a personal data breach, but every personal data breach IS a security incident.
Article 34: Notification to the EDPS
When: In the case of a personal data breach, the controller shall without undue delay and, where feasible, not later than 72 hours after having become aware of it, notify the EDPS. Where notification is not made within 72 hours, it shall be accompanied by reasons for the delay.
Exception: The notification is not required if the breach is unlikely to result in a risk to the rights and freedoms of natural persons.
Content of notification (Art. 34(3)):
(a) The nature of the breach, including the categories and approximate number of data subjects and records concerned.
(b) The name and contact details of the DPO or other contact point.
(c) The likely consequences of the breach.
(d) The measures taken or proposed to address the breach, including measures to mitigate its possible adverse effects.
Phased notification: Information may be provided in phases without undue further delay.
Internal record (Art. 34(5)): The controller must document all personal data breaches, including facts, effects, and remedial action — even if no notification was sent to the EDPS.
Article 35: Communication to the Data Subject
When: When the breach is likely to result in a high risk to the rights and freedoms of natural persons, the controller shall communicate the breach to the data subject without undue delay.
Content (Art. 35(2)): In clear and plain language, describe: the nature of the breach, the DPO contact, the likely consequences, and the measures taken or proposed.
Exceptions (Art. 35(3)):
(a) The controller has implemented appropriate technical protection measures (e.g., encryption) that render the data unintelligible.
(b) The controller has taken subsequent measures which ensure that the high risk is no longer likely to materialise.
(c) It would involve disproportionate effort — in which case a public communication or similar measure may be used instead.
Article 33: Processor Obligations
A processor shall notify the controller without undue delay after becoming aware of a personal data breach.
Key implication: The processor’s notification starts the clock for the controller. The controller cannot delay by waiting for “complete information.”
2. EDPS Guidelines: Key Principles
Summary of EDPS Guidelines on Personal Data Breach Notification (21 November 2018) and EDPB Guidelines 9/2022.
2.1 Assessing Risk to Data Subjects
The assessment must be objective and focus on the likelihood and severity of the risk to the rights and freedoms of data subjects. It is NOT about risk to the organisation.
Factors to consider:
Factor
Description
Type of breach
Confidentiality breaches where data is in the hands of an unauthorised third party are generally higher risk than pure availability breaches.
Nature and sensitivity of data
Special categories (health, political opinions, biometric, genetic) and financial data significantly increase risk. Context matters: professional emails are less sensitive than health records.
Ease of identification
Directly identifiable data (name + email + ID) vs. pseudonymised data. Pseudonymisation reduces but does not eliminate risk.
Severity of consequences
Consider: identity theft, financial loss, discrimination, reputational damage, physical/psychological harm, loss of control over personal data.
Volume
The number of affected individuals. Large-scale breaches create systemic risk.
Special characteristics of individuals
Vulnerable persons (children, patients, employees, whistleblowers) face higher risk from the same breach.
Special characteristics of the controller
Considerations specific to the controller’s role and relationship with data subjects.
Number of affected individuals
Even a breach affecting one person can be high risk if the data is extremely sensitive.

2.3 When Pseudonymisation and Encryption Help
Encryption and pseudonymisation are mitigating factors, not silver bullets:
If data is encrypted with a state-of-the-art algorithm and the key has not been compromised, this may reduce the breach to “unlikely risk.”
If the encryption key is compromised, lost, or accessible to the attacker, encryption provides no protection.
Pseudonymised data is still personal data under the EUDPR. The existence of a re-identification key (even if separate and secure) means the data remains identifiable.
A motivated, well-resourced attacker may attempt re-identification through alternative means (quasi-identifiers, auxiliary datasets).
3. Cybersecurity Regulation: Key Provisions
Regulation (EU, Euratom) 2023/2841 — provisions relevant to personal data breach management.
This Regulation lays down measures for a high common level of cybersecurity at Union entities. It creates obligations that intersect with and complement the EUDPR’s breach notification requirements.
Key Roles
Role
Responsibility
Local Cybersecurity Officer (LCO)
Designated by each EUIBA. Responsible for implementing the Regulation, reporting incidents to CERT-EU, and coordinating with the DPO on incidents involving personal data.
CERT-EU
The Computer Emergency Response Team for EU institutions. Coordinates incident response across EUIBAs. Classifies incidents by severity.
Key Obligations
Significant incidents must be reported to CERT-EU without undue delay (Article 21).
The LCO must coordinate with CERT-EU for incidents classified as “significant” under Article 19.
When a cybersecurity incident involves personal data, the LCO must coordinate with the DPO to ensure EUDPR obligations are also met.
Information shared with CERT-EU covers: nature of the incident, impact assessment, containment measures, and potential cross-border effects.
During the PEDRRA exercise: Pay attention to the interplay between the LCO’s CERT-EU reporting obligations and the DPO’s EDPS notification obligations. They run in parallel, not sequentially.

5. Role-Specific Information Sheets
Review the sheet for your primary professional role. During the exercise, you may be assigned a different role — these sheets help you understand what each role brings to the table.
5.1 For IT Security Personnel / LCOs
Your Perspective in Breach Management
What you bring: Technical assessment of the incident. Understanding of security controls, their effectiveness, and their limitations.
Key questions you should ask: 
• What security measures were in place, and did they function as intended?
• What is the scope of the compromise (systems, data, duration)?
• Can we confirm whether data was accessed, copied, or merely exposed?
• What is the threat actor’s likely capability and motivation?
• What containment measures can we take immediately?
Common pitfall: Overstating the protection provided by technical measures (e.g., “it’s encrypted, so it’s fine”). Encryption mitigates risk; it does not eliminate it.
5.2 For Data Protection Officers
Your Perspective in Breach Management
What you bring: Legal assessment of the breach. Notification obligations. Risk assessment from the data subject’s perspective.
Key questions you should ask: 
• Is this a personal data breach under Art. 3(16)? What type(s)?
• What is the risk to data subjects’ rights and freedoms?
• When did the controller become “aware”? When does the 72-hour clock start?
• Do we need to notify the EDPS? Communicate to data subjects?
• Is the risk assessment documented and defensible?
Common pitfall: Accepting the IT team’s assessment at face value without independently evaluating the risk to data subjects.
5.3 For Legal Teams / Senior Management
Your Perspective in Breach Management
What you bring: Strategic decision-making. Accountability. Organisational context. Contractual and regulatory compliance.
Key questions you should ask: 
• Can we defend this risk assessment to the EDPS?
• What are our contractual obligations (DPA compliance)?
• What is our accountability position if this goes badly?
• Are there additional violations exposed by this breach (retention, documentation)?
• What is the reputational and institutional impact?
Common pitfall: Pressure to classify as “unlikely risk” to avoid notification. The EDPS may later disagree, and the accountability consequences of under-notification are severe.

A.3 Facilitation Notes & Watch Points
⚠ Watch Point 1: “Encryption = No Risk” Bias
Some teams will conclude that full-disk encryption eliminates all risk. Challenge them:
• What if the passphrase is eventually brute-forced? BitLocker is strong, but not unbreakable over time.
• The thief has unlimited physical access. No time pressure. They can try at their leisure.
• Encryption protects data at rest, but the device is permanently out of the controller’s hands.
Reinforce: encryption is a mitigating factor, not an absolute shield.
⚠ Watch Point 2: The 72-Hour Awareness Trigger
Question 3 of Block 1 asks when the EUIBA becomes “aware.” Expect debate. Three candidate moments:
09:45 — Consultant calls Head of Unit (first knowledge of theft, but no confirmation of personal data)
10:00 — Head of Unit informs IT Security (institutional knowledge, investigation begins)
10:30 — IT confirms personal data was on the device (reasonable certainty of personal data compromise)
EDPS Guidelines: “awareness” = reasonable degree of certainty that personal data is compromised. Push teams to justify their answer with reference to this standard. Most defensible answer: 10:30 UTC.
⚠ Watch Point 3: Remote Wipe as a Safety Net
Before the inject, teams may rely on the pending remote wipe as a safety net.
After the inject confirms the wipe has NOT executed, watch for teams that still cling to “it might connect later.”
Push them: How long do you wait? At what point does “pending” become “failed” for risk assessment purposes?
Real-world lesson: you cannot base a risk assessment on an outcome you hope for but cannot confirm.
⚠ Watch Point 4: Data Scope Creep After Inject
The inject reveals phone numbers were also in the dataset. This is designed to:
• Increase identifiability (phone number + name = easy to contact/target individuals)
• Show how data scope can expand during an investigation
• Test whether teams update their assessment dynamically
Teams that had concluded “Unlikely Risk” should reconsider after the inject.

A.5 Debrief Extension Questions (Plenary Only)
Use these only if time permits during the plenary discussion. They extend the scenario into related areas.
Extension 1 — Unencrypted Data: “If the laptop had contained unencrypted personal data, how would this change your assessment? Would this constitute High Risk?”

Extension 2 — No Backups: “If the laptop was the sole copy of the data (no backups existed), what additional type of breach would this represent?”
Expected: Availability breach in addition to the confidentiality breach. This would increase severity significantly.
Extension 3 — Ransomware Alternative: “Imagine instead of a theft, EU Connect suffered a ransomware attack on its internal servers. (a) Could this be an availability breach? Step 2: Head of Unit → IT Security team (already done at 10:00). Key info: request for immediate technical assessment.
Step 3: IT Security → LCO (immediately after initial assessment). Key info: confirmed security incident, personal data potentially compromised, remote wipe pending.
Step 4: IT Security / LCO → DPO (within hours of confirming personal data was on the device). Key info: nature of data, number of subjects, security measures in place, assessment of risk.
Step 5: DPO → Senior Management (same day). Key info: breach classification, preliminary risk assessment, potential notification obligations.
Step 6: DPO → Legal (if High Risk or complex questions arise). Key info: notification obligations, contractual implications with the consultant.
Key point: The DPO must be involved as soon as personal data is confirmed to be on the device (10:30 UTC). Delay beyond this point risks the 72-hour clock.
Question 3: 72-Hour Awareness Trigger
Model Answer
Most defensible answer: 10:30 UTC (when IT Security confirms personal data was on the device).
Reasoning: The EDPS Guidelines clarify that “awareness” begins when the controller has a “reasonable degree of certainty” that a security incident has resulted in personal data being compromised. At 09:45, the Head of Unit knows about the theft but not about personal data. At 10:00, IT Security begins investigating. At 10:30, the investigation confirms personal data was present on the device.
Acceptable alternative: Teams may argue 10:00 UTC on the basis that the Head of Unit already knew the consultant worked on the UnionGate project and therefore data was likely present. This is a defensible position if well-argued.
Not acceptable: Arguing awareness begins only when the DPO is formally informed. The controller is “aware” through its employees/agents, regardless of internal notification delays.
E.2 Model Answers — Block 2: Breach Identification
Question 4: Is This a Personal Data Breach?
Model Answer
Yes, this is a personal data breach as defined under Article 3(16) EUDPR.
Confidentiality breach: YES. An unauthorised third party (the thief) has physical possession of a device containing personal data. Even though the data is encrypted, the potential for unauthorised access exists. The EDPS Guidelines consider loss of physical control a confidentiality breach, mitigated but not eliminated by encryption.
Integrity breach: NO. There is no evidence of unauthorised alteration of the data.
Availability breach: NO (for the controller). EU Connect has backups. However, if backups did NOT exist, this would also constitute an availability breach.
Question 5: Consultant’s Status
Model Answer
The consultant acts as a processor under the DPA. The DPA exists and explicitly governs the consultant’s data handling obligations, including encryption and incident notification.
Key implication: The processor (consultant) has notified the controller (EU Connect) of the incident, fulfilling their Article 33(2) obligation. EU Connect, as controller, must now assess the breach and decide on notification to the EDPS.
The DPA’s existence strengthens the assessment: the encryption was contractually required and was in place. However, the pending deletion (data should have been deleted but wasn’t) raises an accountability question about whether the consultant complied with the DPA’s data minimisation requirements.
E.3 Model Answers — Block 3: Risk Assessment
Question 6: Risk Verdict (Before Inject)
Model Answer (Pre-Inject)
Defensible verdict: UNLIKELY RISK (leaning towards Risk).
The combination of strong encryption + strong passphrase + opportunistic theft + backups + non-sensitive data categories creates a strong case that this is “unlikely to result in a risk to the rights and freedoms” of the data subjects. However, the pending remote wipe introduces uncertainty.
Note: Teams that argue “Risk” pre-inject are also correct if they emphasise the permanent loss of physical control. The point is that this is genuinely ambiguous — that’s the pedagogical design.

E.4 Model Risk Assessment (Post-Inject)
Overall Assessment: RISK (Notification to EDPS Required)
Risk Level: RISK (not Unlikely Risk, not High Risk).
Rationale: After the inject, the balance shifts. The confirmed failure of the remote wipe means the only protection is encryption. While BitLocker with a strong passphrase provides robust protection, the controller can no longer guarantee data destruction. The addition of phone numbers increases identifiability and the potential for targeted contact.
Notification to EDPS: YES (required under Article 34 EUDPR, within 72 hours of awareness).
Communication to data subjects: NOT REQUIRED. The encryption remains effective and the data is not in the special categories. Key Pedagogical Takeaway
This scenario is deliberately ambiguous. The “correct” answer is not a fixed verdict but a well-reasoned assessment that weighs all factors.
The core lesson: encryption significantly reduces risk but does not eliminate it when physical control is permanently lost. The controller must still assess, document, and (in most post-inject interpretations) notify the EDPS.
Teams that reach different verdicts but demonstrate rigorous reasoning should be acknowledged. The process matters as much as the conclusion.

A.3 Facilitation Notes & Watch Points
⚠ Watch Point 1: The Health Status Indicator Debate
The health status indicator (Good/Fair/Poor) is the pivotal element. Teams WILL debate whether this constitutes “data concerning health” under Article 10 EUDPR.
The correct position: EUDPR/GDPR adopts a BROAD interpretation. Per Recital 35 of the GDPR, “data concerning health” includes data from which information about health status can be inferred. Even a generalised indicator reveals something about the person’s health.
Expect pushback: “But it’s pseudonymised!” — yes, but pseudonymisation is not anonymisation. The re-identification key exists (held by EUIBA-Beta). A motivated state-sponsored actor could potentially re-identify individuals through quasi-identifiers.
Let the debate run. This is the learning moment. Intervene only to steer back to the legal text.
⚠ Watch Point 2: The 72-Hour Clock (Three Candidate Timestamps)
Question 4 of Block 1 forces teams to pick when the clock starts. Three candidates:
14:00 UTC — CERT-EU alert. The controller knows of a major incident at its processor, but not yet that its own data is affected.
15:30 UTC — CloudVault’s direct notification. The processor explicitly tells the controller about the breach.
19:00 UTC — EUIBA-Alpha confirms its specific data was in the affected storage buckets.
Most defensible: 15:30 UTC. At this point the controller has been directly informed by its processor that the breach affects their infrastructure. Even without confirmed data scope, the controller should have “reasonable certainty” that personal data may be compromised.
Acceptable alternative: 19:00 UTC, if the team argues that 15:30 gave knowledge of a processor breach but not confirmation that EUIBA-Alpha’s specific data was affected. This is defensible but riskier.
⚠ Watch Point 3: Pseudonymisation Is Not Anonymisation
Many teams will over-rely on pseudonymisation as a shield. Key points to probe:
• The re-identification key exists and could theoretically be obtained (though EUIBA-Beta’s systems are not compromised).
• A state-sponsored actor with advanced capabilities may attempt re-identification through quasi-identifiers (age group + gender + country).
• Research shows that combinations of 3+ demographic attributes can re-identify individuals with high accuracy, especially for smaller Member States.
• EUDPR treats pseudonymised data as personal data (Recital 17 GDPR).
⚠ Watch Point 4: Processor Accountability — “Not Our Fault”
Some teams will argue that the encryption failure is CloudVault’s fault, not EUIBA-Alpha’s.
Push back: The controller chose CloudVault, signed the DPA, and has an obligation to verify compliance. The DPIA (overdue for review by a year) should have triggered a reassessment of processor security. The missing encryption raises questions about due diligence.
Key teaching point: A controller cannot fully delegate accountability. Article 29 EUDPR imposes obligations on both parties. The processor’s failure compounds the controller’s own accountability gap.
⚠ Watch Point 5: Data Retention Oversight
The 7-year-old pilot data (2 years beyond retention) is a pre-existing violation that the breach has exposed.
• This is a separate violation of the storage limitation principle (Art. 4(1)(e) EUDPR).
• It compounds the breach: more data was exposed than should have existed.
• It must be addressed in the EDPS notification as an additional finding.
• The automated deletion script failure suggests systemic data governance weaknesses.
⚠ Watch Point 6: Post-Inject Escalation (Free-Text Notes)
The inject reveals 500 records with undocumented free-text notes containing medical conditions, disability status, and political opinions.
This is the tipping point. Before the inject, the “Risk vs. High Risk” debate is genuine. After the inject, the case for High Risk becomes very strong:
• Undocumented Article 10 special categories (health, political opinions) were processed without basis.
• These were not in the RoPA or the DPIA — a separate accountability failure.
• Even with pseudonymisation, specific medical conditions + demographic data significantly increase re-identification risk.
Watch for teams that had firmly argued “Risk” — they must now reassess under new evidence. This simulates a real-world evolving breach investigation.

A.5 Practical Examples Session Guide
Use these three examples in the session that follows this scenario (14:30–15:00). They reinforce risk level distinctions.
Example 1: Accidental Email Disclosure to Recruitment Candidates
Scenario: A member of HR accidentally sends an email to all rejected candidates with email addresses in the CC field instead of BCC.
Classification: Confidentiality breach. Risk level: Risk (not High Risk). Notification to EDPS required. Communication to data subjects not required — the data disclosed (application status) is sensitive but not in the special categories, and the recipients are limited to the applicant pool.
Example 2: Whistleblowing Database Hacked and Published
Scenario: A database containing whistleblower identities and persons concerned has been hacked and published online.
Classification: Confidentiality breach. Risk level: High Risk. Notification to EDPS AND communication to data subjects required. The highly sensitive nature (whistleblower identity) and public disclosure create severe risk of retaliation, professional harm, and personal safety concerns.
Example 3: Lost Encrypted USB Stick
Scenario: A senior official loses a USB stick containing draft decisions with personal data. The USB is encrypted with a state-of-the-art algorithm. Backup exists.
Classification: Potential confidentiality breach. Risk level: Unlikely Risk (if encryption key is not compromised and data can be restored). Notification to EDPS and communication to data subjects may not be required. However, the risk must be re-evaluated if the encryption key is later compromised.

B.3 Detailed Technical Findings (Day 1, 17:00 UTC)
Compromise Scope
Attackers gained unauthorised access to specific storage buckets on CloudVault used by EUIBA-Alpha for the public health research project.
Attack vector: a sophisticated spear-phishing campaign targeting CloudVault system administrators, which led to compromised administrative credentials.
Data exfiltration occurred over a 48-hour period prior to detection. The attackers used encrypted channels to extract data, making it difficult to determine the exact volume exfiltrated.
Attribution: CERT-EU’s analysis suggests the attack was conducted by a suspected state-sponsored cyber-espionage group known to target EU institutional data.
Data Involved
The compromised database contains 750,000 records of EU citizens participating in the research study. Each record includes:

Classification
Pseudonymised identifier
Generated by a one-way cryptographic hash of the participant’s national ID number

Values: “Good,” “Fair,” “Poor” — derived from self-reported general health questionnaires

Re-identification Key

No evidence of any compromise of EUIBA-Beta’s systems or the key itself.

Research has shown that combinations of three or more demographic attributes can re-identify individuals with varying degrees of success, particularly for residents of smaller EU Member States.
Contractual Breach by Processor
Critical Finding
Encryption at rest was NOT enabled for the affected storage buckets, despite being a mandatory requirement under clause 5.2(a) of the DPA and a condition identified as essential in EUIBA-Alpha’s DPIA.
CloudVault has not yet provided an explanation for this discrepancy.
CloudVault’s most recent independent security audit (8 months ago) did not flag this configuration gap.
Data Retention Oversight
Critical Finding
EUIBA-Alpha’s data retention policy specifies a maximum storage period of 5 years.
Internal checks reveal that data from the project’s pilot phase, dating back 7 years, was still present in the compromised database due to an oversight in automated deletion scripts.
This means an additional 2 years of historical data, including health status indicators, were exposed beyond their intended retention period.
This constitutes a potential violation of the storage limitation principle under Article 4(1)(e) EUDPR, compounding the breach with an accountability issue that pre-dates the security incident.
Key Factors Summary
Aggravating: 750,000 records; health data (Art. 10); state-sponsored attacker with advanced capabilities; encryption at rest missing (processor failure); data retention violation; 48-hour undetected exfiltration; 27 Member States affected.
Mitigating: Data is pseudonymised; re-identification key is on a separate, uncompromised system; demographic data alone has limited re-identification potential; no evidence of key compromise.

E.1 Model Answers — Block 1: Initial Response & Communication
Question 1: Immediate Internal Steps
Model Answer
1. LCO immediately coordinates with CERT-EU (Cybersecurity Regulation obligation).
2. IT Security initiates containment: review access logs for CloudVault connections, revoke or rotate any API keys or credentials used to access the cloud storage.
3. LCO informs the DPO of the incident (same day, as soon as personal data involvement is suspected).
4. DPO initiates the breach assessment process: confirms scope of data, reviews the DPA, contacts CloudVault for detailed forensics.
5. DPO briefs Senior Management and Legal on the incident, preliminary assessment, and likely notification obligations.
6. DPO contacts EUIBA-Beta to request heightened security monitoring of the re-identification key system.
7. IT Security requests CloudVault’s full forensic report and engages CERT-EU’s technical support.
8. DPO begins drafting the EDPS notification (even if preliminary; information may be provided in phases).
Question 2: Cybersecurity Regulation Obligations
Model Answer
Under Regulation (EU, Euratom) 2023/2841, EUIBA-Alpha must:
• Respond to the CERT-EU alert and cooperate with the coordination process for significant incidents (Art. 19).
• The LCO must provide CERT-EU with: nature and scope of the incident, initial technical assessment, containment measures taken, and potential cross-border impact.
• Timeframe: without undue delay after becoming aware. The LCO should respond to CERT-EU the same day (Day 1).
• The LCO must also inform EUIBA-Alpha’s own management and coordinate with the DPO regarding the intersection of cybersecurity incident handling and personal data breach management.
Question 3: Communication with EUIBA-Beta
Model Answer
(a) Securing the key: Priority 1. EUIBA-Alpha should immediately notify EUIBA-Beta to: verify the integrity of its re-identification key system, implement enhanced monitoring, and confirm no unauthorised access.
(b) Nature of compromised data: Yes. EUIBA-Beta needs to understand what was compromised to assess the risk of re-identification attempts against its key system.
(c) Who initiates: EUIBA-Alpha (as controller) initiates. Communication should go through the DPO or senior management channel established in the inter-institutional agreement. This should be done urgently via secure channel.
(d) EUIBA-Beta’s actions: Enhanced monitoring, access audits, consider temporarily restricting access to the re-identification system as a precaution.
Question 4: 72-Hour Awareness Trigger
Model Answer
Most defensible: 15:30 UTC (CloudVault’s direct notification).
At 14:00, the LCO knows of an incident at a processor but not that EUIBA-Alpha’s data is affected. At 15:30, the processor directly notifies the controller that its infrastructure has been breached, invoking both the EUDPR and the DPA. At this point, the controller has a “reasonable degree of certainty” that personal data may be compromised.
Acceptable alternative: 19:00 UTC, if the team argues that 15:30 provided knowledge of a general processor breach, but confirmation of EUIBA-Alpha’s specific data involvement only came at 19:00. This is defensible but more conservative.
Not acceptable: 14:00 UTC as the sole trigger. At this point the alert is about CloudVault generally, not about EUIBA-Alpha’s data specifically. However, 14:00 should trigger the investigation that leads to awareness.
E.2 Model Answers — Block 2: Breach Classification
Question 5: Is This a Personal Data Breach?
Model Answer
Yes, this is a personal data breach.
Confidentiality breach: YES. Unauthorised access to and exfiltration of personal data by an external threat actor. Data was accessed and copied without authorisation.
Integrity breach: NO. No evidence that data was altered. However, this should be confirmed through forensic analysis.
Availability breach: NO. EUIBA-Alpha retains access to its data through CloudVault (the breach does not destroy or deny access). However, trust in the processor’s environment is compromised, which may require data migration.
Question 6: Effect of Pseudonymisation
Model Answer
Pseudonymised data IS still personal data under the EUDPR (Recital 17 GDPR). Pseudonymisation is a security measure, not anonymisation.
Key points:
• The re-identification key exists (held by EUIBA-Beta) and could theoretically be obtained.
• Even without the key, quasi-identifiers (age + gender + country) provide a pathway to re-identification, especially for smaller Member States.
• The threat actor is a suspected state-sponsored group with advanced capabilities — they may have access to auxiliary datasets for cross-referencing.
Pseudonymisation is a mitigating factor in the risk assessment, but it does not change the classification of the data as personal data or the incident as a personal data breach.
Question 7: Health Status Indicator
Model Answer
Yes, the health status indicator constitutes “data concerning health” within the meaning of Article 10 EUDPR and Recital 35 GDPR.
The EUDPR/GDPR adopts a BROAD interpretation: “data concerning health” includes any data from which information about an individual’s health status can be inferred. A “Good/Fair/Poor” indicator, even if derived from a general questionnaire, reveals information about the person’s health.
Anticipate debate: Some participants will argue that such a generalised indicator, when pseudonymised, reveals almost nothing. Acknowledge this as a fair practical point, but steer back to the legal position: the definition is intentionally broad to protect data subjects.
Question 8: Data Retention Oversight
Model Answer
Yes, this is a separate data protection violation.
• Storage limitation violation: Article 4(1)(e) EUDPR requires data to be kept no longer than necessary. The 5-year retention period was breached by 2 years.
• Accountability failure: The automated deletion script failure suggests inadequate data governance processes.
• Compounding effect: More data was exposed than should have existed. This increases the severity of the breach and must be reported to the EDPS.
• Separate from the breach: This violation pre-dates the CloudVault compromise. The breach exposed it, but it exists independently.
E.3 Model Answers — Block 3: Risk Assessment
Question 9: Risk Verdict
Model Answer: PRE-INJECT — Genuine Debate Between Risk and High Risk
Before the inject, this is a legitimate debate. Both positions are defensible:
Arguments for RISK (not High Risk): Data is pseudonymised with the key on a separate, uncompromised system. The health indicator is generalised. Re-identification through quasi-identifiers alone is impractical at scale. No evidence the attacker can reverse the pseudonymisation.
Arguments for HIGH RISK: The data includes health information (Art. 10). 750,000 records across 27 Member States. State-sponsored attacker with advanced capabilities and potential access to auxiliary datasets. Encryption at rest was missing. Data retention violation expanded the exposure.
Model Answer: POST-INJECT — Strong Case for High Risk
After the inject, the balance shifts decisively towards HIGH RISK.
The discovery of 500 records with undocumented medical conditions, disability status, and political opinions changes the analysis fundamentally:
• These are unambiguously Article 10 special categories.
• They are not pseudonymised in the same way (free-text with specific conditions is far more re-identifiable than “Good/Fair/Poor”).
• The undocumented nature means they were processed without a proper legal basis, RoPA entry, or DPIA coverage.
• Combined with demographic quasi-identifiers, specific medical conditions could enable targeted re-identification of vulnerable individuals.
Teams that maintain “Risk” after the inject must explain how 500 records with specific medical conditions and political opinions, accessible to a state-sponsored actor, do not meet the “high risk to the rights and freedoms” threshold.

Question 11: Notification Obligations
Model Answer
(a) EDPS (Art. 34): YES. Required regardless of whether the verdict is Risk or High Risk. Must be filed within 72 hours of awareness. Information may be provided in phases.
(b) CERT-EU: YES. Already triggered by the CERT-EU alert. The LCO must provide ongoing updates as the investigation progresses.

(d) Other supervisory authorities: POSSIBLY. If data subjects reside in multiple Member States and GDPR authorities need to be informed, EUIBA-Alpha should coordinate with the EDPS on this.

Question 12: Data Subject Communication
Model Answer — Key Elements
Nature of breach: “We have been informed that a security incident at a service provider used for a research project may have resulted in unauthorised access to data collected as part of [project name]. The affected data includes general health information and demographic details, protected by pseudonymisation.”
DPO contact: [Name, email, phone of EUIBA-Alpha’s DPO]

What you can do: “Monitor for unusual contacts referencing this research project. Be cautious of unsolicited communications claiming to be from EU institutions. Report concerns to [DPO contact].”
Delivery challenge: 750,000 individuals across 27 Member States requires: multilingual communication (at minimum the 24 EU official languages), coordination with national health authorities who may have participant contact details, potentially public announcement if individual notification is disproportionately difficult (Art. 35(3)(c) EUDPR).
Question 13: Processor Accountability
Model Answer
CloudVault’s accountability: 
• DPA breach (clause 5.2(a)): encryption at rest was a contractual obligation. CloudVault must explain the discrepancy.
• Audit failure: the independent audit (8 months ago) missed this configuration gap, raising questions about audit quality.
• EUIBA-Alpha should consider: contractual penalties, review of all other data held by CloudVault, potential termination and data migration, and formal complaint to relevant authorities.
EUIBA-Alpha’s own accountability: 
• The controller chose CloudVault and signed the DPA. Article 29 EUDPR requires sufficient guarantees.
• The DPIA review was overdue by approximately 1 year. A timely review might have identified the encryption gap.
• The data retention oversight is entirely EUIBA-Alpha’s responsibility, not CloudVault’s.
• The undocumented free-text fields were added by EUIBA-Alpha’s own research coordinators, not by CloudVault.
Key teaching point: The controller cannot fully delegate accountability. The processor’s failure does not relieve the controller of its obligations.

Overall Assessment: HIGH RISK (Post-Inject)
Risk Level: HIGH RISK.
Rationale: The combination of: (1) health data (Article 10 special category); (2) 750,000 data subjects across 27 Member States; (3) suspected state-sponsored attacker with advanced re-identification capabilities; (4) processor’s contractual failure on encryption; (5) data retention violation expanding exposure scope; and critically (6) the post-inject discovery of undocumented free-text fields containing specific medical conditions and political opinions — together create a scenario that is “likely to result in a high risk to the rights and freedoms” of data subjects.
Notification to EDPS: YES (required, within 72 hours, information in phases).
Communication to data subjects: YES (required, without undue delay).
CERT-EU coordination: YES (ongoing, as per Cybersecurity Regulation).

Key Pedagogical Takeaways for This Scenario
1. Multiple compounding factors shift the balance: no single factor makes this High Risk, but the combination does.
2. Pseudonymisation is a safeguard, not a guarantee. A motivated state-sponsored attacker changes the re-identification calculus.
3. Controllers cannot fully delegate accountability to processors. Due diligence and DPIA reviews are ongoing obligations.
4. Breach investigations evolve. Risk assessments must be updated as new information emerges. The initial classification is not final.
5. Data governance failures (retention, documentation) have real consequences when a breach occurs — they compound the severity.

Why This Pack Matters
Research consistently shows that formal training accounts for roughly 10% of professional learning (the 70:20:10 model).
The other 90% comes from on-the-job experience and social learning.
This pack extends PEDRRA’s impact into that 90% through three mechanisms:
1. A lightweight check-in at 6 weeks to keep the training top of mind.
2. A community of practice for ongoing peer learning.
3. A commitment to deliver a tangible resource within 4 weeks based on participant-identified gaps.

A single-question email sent 6 weeks after the training. Its purposes:
Keep the PEDRRA training top of mind during the critical application window.
Provide an early signal on Level 3 (behaviour change) before the full 3–6 month survey.
Maintain the connection between the EDPS training team and participants.
Identify participants who may need additional support.
